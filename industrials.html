Industrial Sector Memo 1.8.2026

Wrote this as a way of preparing for my interviews

1) What is Industrials? What is the industrials sector, you may ask? Industrials, relating to “industry,” is the part of the economy that builds and runs the physical world. It’s how we create and move everything around us — machines, equipment, aircraft, transportation networks, and the commercial services that keep businesses operating.

2) 2022-2024; Post-COVID Industrials came up slow from 2022 to 2024, but has begun to pick up again starting in 2025. The sector was struggling post-COVID due to higher rates, inflation, tariffs, and valuation gaps. The strategic interest never left, but the numbers weren't adding up to make deals happen, so activity skewed towards smaller, safer transactions. add-ons, portfolio clean-ups, carve-outs, rather than mega deals.

3) 2025 digitalization Fast forward to 2025, industrials sector is rebounding robustly as manufacturers are under real pressured to adopt automation, AI, and IoT services. At first, automation and tech was a helpful supplement, allowing companies to streamline their infrastructure using predictive maintenance, demand forecasting, supply chain planning, etc. But, that discretionary spending is now becoming mandatory to keep up with competition. Securing technology is the new way for companies to scale and take their growth to the next level. A lot of factories, warehouses, etc. are becoming data-rich environments, and the use of AI and tech is genuinely game-changing and necessary.

4) Geopolitics and A&D Second, geopolitical tension has skyrocketed over the last three years. Defense budgets are being raised and millions are being poured into next-gen capabilities like drones, planes, and security systems. Collaborations in cyber, ai, and the electronic warfare are proliferating. For example, Lockheed Martin has announced the tripling the production of one of their missiles through a deal with the Pentagon. Further, Trump mentioned that he's not going to permit defense contractors to issue dividends and stock buybacks because he wants military equipment production ramped up and more spent on CapEx and inventory. In terms of M&A, a new form of R&D is the increase of acquisitions. Companies are looking to acquire developed technologies that align with their goals and and priorities, rather than spending on their own research which takes time.

5) Labor Shortage This has led to the industry being faced head-on with one of their biggest problems, talent location. Majority of engineers and manufacturing workers are older than 50 and nearing retirement. Companies find themselves in a severe labor shortage, and are struggling to hire talent to push forth new technologies. As a result, M&A is becoming a way not just to buy revenue or technology, but to acquire complete teams of engineers, technicians, and domain experts who are hard to recruit in the open market.

Robotics is becoming a crucial part of the industrial story, but it solves a different part of the problem. They're very effective at doing hazardous, repetitive, line work in factories and warehouses, but the convergence of physical AI and the real world isn't making up for the lack of specialized engineering talent. They work alongside humans and are learning to interact with the world. They are constantly adapting to situations and behavior. As they get smarter, they'll be more than capable of working in high-pressure, complex environments. Their ability to perceive and learn is the real capability, they're bridging the gap between the the digital and real world, and doing things humans can't -- predictive maintenance, calculating ideal scheduling, and spotting things that people miss.

Optimizing AI infrastructure is becoming a big problem. Companies have begun to use agentic AI, which is driving massive increases in compute demand and energy consumption, They're getting great use, but these same companies are seeing millions of dollars in their monthly bills. As a result, trillions are being invested into prolific data centers and new infrastructure. However, this will take time. As of now, the key bottleneck is the dissonance between how quickly enterprises are expanding AI use and how long it takes to complete the necessary physical infrastructure.

AI data centers have become a massive buzzword recently, so real quick, what are they?

    AI data centers are specialized infrastructure with high computing powers, built specifically for the use of artificial intelligence processing. They're built to help optimize: 1) Memory 2) Preparing data for AI model consumption 3) High performance networks and reducing latency 4) Optimized software that streamlines AI use cases for specific business uses 5) Unified systems that allow for integration of multiple AI systems They're important for two reasons: 1) The obvious one; AI is becoming the new wave, transforming our technology, streamlining our businesses, and has capabilities that we only dreamed of 2) The affect on industrials; the multi-year large scale investments are creating infrastructure that directly benefits engineering, construction, power, and grid companies. Demand for machinery, components, and advanced manufacturing is skyrocketing.

**Quick thoughts:** McKinsey estimates that over 5 trillion dollars of data-center investments may be needed by 2030 to keep up with our AI compute demand. This creates a real tension between the profitability of these systems and whether they can generate enough cash flow to justify such large capex and associated debt. A really interesting argument is that regardless of what happens with AI, it seems to be transformational, but if it busts slightly worse than expected, those high-powered centers will continue to be the backbone of our infrastructure and can be used for memory, cloud workspaces, and other use cases. The physical assets themselves will have value regardless.
